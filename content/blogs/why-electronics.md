Back in my 11th and 12th grade, I was heavily interested in Computer Science. I spent a lot of time learning programming languages and trying to build small things on my own.

Like most beginners, one of the very first programs I wrote was a simple "Hello, World!" program. Looking back, it was just a few lines of code printing text onto a screen. But at that moment, it felt much bigger than that.

For the first time, I was telling a computer to do something, and it was doing exactly what I asked. It felt like I was communicating with a machine. It was a simple interaction, but it left me fascinated. That curiosity pushed me to learn more and write more programs.

Nowadays, many people start coding at a very young age. My journey started when I took Computer Science in higher secondary school. The more I learned about programming, the more questions I had. Not about the code itself, but about the machine running it.

I knew how to write programs.

But how does a computer understand those programs?

How does a machine know what my code means?

What is actually happening inside the computer when I click "Run"?

These questions stayed in my mind when it was time to choose an engineering branch.

Most people would naturally move from Computer Science in school to Computer Science in engineering. But I decided to take Electronics and Communication Engineering instead.

The reason was simple.

I didn't just want to learn how to tell a computer what to do. I wanted to understand how the computer itself worked.

Electronics seemed like the closest path to that understanding.

When I entered engineering, I started learning about digital electronics, logic gates, processors, communication systems, microcontrollers, and the countless components that work together to make modern computing possible.

One thing that fascinated me the most was how everything eventually comes down to something surprisingly simple.

Computers do not understand the complexity that we see. They do not understand websites, videos, applications, or even programming languages the way humans do. At their core, they only understand two states: 0 and 1, OFF and ON.

Yet somehow, billions of transistors switching between these two states are capable of creating everything from calculators to smartphones and supercomputers.

That idea never stopped amazing me.

When you think about it, computers have come a long way. What once occupied entire rooms now fits inside our pockets. The smartphone that we carry every day is more powerful than computers that once filled buildings.

Understanding the engineering behind that evolution made me appreciate technology even more.

At the same time, electronics taught me something else.

The field is enormous.

The first year gives you an overview of what electronics is, but after that, there are countless paths you can follow—embedded systems, microcontrollers, VLSI, communication systems, real-time systems, hardware design, and many more.

The deeper I went, the more I realized how much there was to learn.

Eventually, after understanding the fundamentals of how computers work, I found myself naturally returning to software.

Not because I had lost interest in electronics.

But because building things through code was still what excited me the most.

The difference was that this time, I wasn't just writing programs. I had a better understanding of what was happening underneath the layers of abstraction.

I had learned how the machine worked before going back to telling the machine what to do.

And that is why I chose Electronics instead of Computer Science.

It wasn't a decision to move away from programming.

It was a decision to understand the foundation beneath it.

Because sometimes, understanding how something works makes you appreciate even more what you can do with it.
